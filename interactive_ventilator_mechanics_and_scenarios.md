# Interactive Ventilator Module: Mechanics, Physiology, and Scenario Design

This document is for the coding AI agent and defines the **mechanics**, **teaching logic**, and **interaction rules** that should govern the Interactive Ventilator tab.

It is intentionally focused on **how variables influence one another** so that the simulation teaches students the clinically meaningful relationships they are expected to know in anesthesia.

---

## Purpose of This Layer

The UI already defines what the monitors look like.

This document defines:
- how waveforms should change
- how vitals should respond
- how ventilator settings interact with patient physiology
- how pressure-volume loops should behave
- how common anesthesia scenarios should unfold
- how multiple-choice teaching prompts should appear
- how student actions should alter the patient state

The result should feel like an **interactive anesthesia learning system**, not only a visual dashboard.

---

## Core Teaching Principle

Every interaction should teach one of the following concepts:

1. **Ventilator changes alter mechanics**
2. **Patient physiology alters mechanics**
3. **Mechanics alter gas exchange and hemodynamics**
4. **Clinical events appear first as waveform/vital changes**
5. **Correct intervention changes the monitors in the right direction**

The simulation does not need to be a full ICU-grade model, but it must preserve the **correct directional physiology**.

# 1. Required Ventilator Domains

The ventilator monitor should include:

- pressure vs time waveform
- flow vs time waveform
- volume vs time waveform
- **pressure-volume loop**
- numeric ventilator measurements
- adjustable ventilator settings
- alarms
- patient state controls

The pressure-volume loop should be added to the ventilator screen as a dedicated panel.

# 2. Core Variables in the Simulation

The simulation should be built around the following groups of variables.

## A. Ventilator setting variables
These are directly controlled by the user.

- mode
  - volume control
  - pressure control
- set tidal volume
- inspiratory pressure
- respiratory rate
- inspiratory time
- inspiratory flow
- FiO2
- PEEP
- optional I:E ratio
- optional inspiratory pause
- optional sevo / anesthetic depth target

## B. Measured ventilator variables
These are generated by the model.

- exhaled tidal volume
- minute ventilation
- peak inspiratory pressure
- plateau pressure
- mean airway pressure
- auto-PEEP if present
- ETCO2
- delivered FiO2
- leak percentage

## C. Patient mechanics variables
These are part of the patient state layer.

- compliance
- airway resistance
- spontaneous effort on/off
- metabolic CO2 production
- leak
- bronchospasm severity
- chest wall stiffness
- obesity / abdominal pressure effect
- circulating volume / preload state

## D. Hemodynamic / vital sign variables
These affect or reflect the patient.

- heart rate
- arterial blood pressure
- NIBP
- SpO2
- ETCO2
- temperature
- BIS
- anesthetic gas concentration
- MAC
- end-tidal oxygen
- sevo concentration

# 3. Foundational Mechanical Relationships

The simulation should preserve the following principles.

## A. Airway pressure equation
Use the simplified relationship:

**Pressure = Volume / Compliance + Flow × Resistance + PEEP**

Interpretation:
- the **elastic component** is driven by compliance
- the **resistive component** is driven by flow and airway resistance
- baseline pressure is set by PEEP

## B. Compliance
Low compliance means the lung is stiff.

Effects:
- higher plateau pressure
- higher peak pressure
- lower delivered volume in pressure control
- narrower pressure-volume loop
- reduced chest expansion conceptually
- possible fall in oxygenation if severe

Examples:
- obesity
- abdominal insufflation
- pulmonary edema
- atelectasis
- ARDS-like state

## C. Resistance
High resistance means the airways are hard to move gas through.

Effects:
- higher peak pressure
- plateau pressure may be relatively less elevated
- larger difference between peak and plateau
- slower expiratory flow decay
- flow may not return to zero before next breath
- air trapping / auto-PEEP may develop
- ETCO2 may rise if severe and minute ventilation falls

Examples:
- bronchospasm
- kinked tube
- secretions
- small ETT
- partial obstruction

## D. Minute ventilation and CO2
Minute ventilation is the major driver of CO2 clearance.

**Minute ventilation = exhaled tidal volume × respiratory rate**

If minute ventilation falls:
- ETCO2 tends to rise

If minute ventilation rises:
- ETCO2 tends to fall

But ETCO2 is also influenced by:
- metabolic CO2 production
- perfusion / cardiac output
- dead space
- severe obstruction
- disconnection / arrest states

## E. Oxygenation
SpO2 and oxygen reserve should respond more slowly than waveforms.

SpO2 should be influenced by:
- FiO2
- alveolar ventilation
- airway obstruction
- apnea
- severe bronchospasm
- disconnect
- anaphylaxis or low perfusion
- severe hypotension
- atelectasis / low compliance

Desaturation should not happen instantly in every scenario. It should be delayed in plausible ways unless the event is severe.

# 4. Waveform Behavior Rules

## A. Pressure vs Time

### Normal appearance
- rises during inspiration
- returns toward PEEP during expiration
- plateau may be visible with inspiratory pause
- baseline remains at PEEP

### If compliance worsens
- peak pressure rises
- plateau pressure rises
- pressure curve sits higher for a given volume

### If resistance worsens
- peak pressure rises
- plateau increases less than peak
- resistive spike more pronounced
- expiratory issues are seen more strongly on the flow waveform

### If PEEP increases
- whole pressure tracing shifts upward
- mean airway pressure rises

### In pressure control
- pressure rises to a target and stays relatively square / flat during inspiration
- volume depends on compliance and resistance

### In volume control
- delivered volume is preserved more strongly
- pressure changes more in response to patient mechanics

---

## B. Flow vs Time

### Normal appearance
- inspiratory flow positive
- expiratory flow negative
- expiratory flow decays smoothly toward zero before the next breath

### If resistance increases
- expiratory flow decays more slowly
- may fail to return to zero before next breath
- indicates air trapping / dynamic hyperinflation

### If inspiratory flow increases
- inspiratory flow peak becomes taller
- resistive pressure component rises
- peak pressure may increase

### If RR increases too much in obstructive physiology
- expiratory flow fails to return to zero
- auto-PEEP may increase

### If leak occurs
- flow behavior may appear abnormal and exhaled volume falls

---

## C. Volume vs Time

### Normal appearance
- rises during inspiration
- falls back toward zero during expiration

### If leak is present
- exhaled volume is lower than inspired volume
- curve may not fully match expected return

### In pressure control
- delivered volume changes with compliance/resistance

### In volume control
- set volume is delivered more consistently unless severe alarms/disconnect occur

---

## D. Pressure-Volume Loop

Add a dedicated pressure-volume loop panel on the ventilator screen.

### What it should show
- x-axis: volume
- y-axis: pressure
- inspiratory limb
- expiratory limb
- hysteresis loop

### Normal interpretation
- loop proceeds with inspiration and expiration in real time
- width and area should look plausible
- inspiratory and expiratory limbs are distinct

### Low compliance
- loop shifts upward
- higher pressure required for the same volume
- loop appears steeper / narrower in terms of volume gained per pressure increment

### Increased resistance
- loop may broaden somewhat and dynamic filling may look less efficient
- the pressure burden rises, especially during inspiration

### Overdistension
- upper inspiratory limb begins to flatten or “beak”
- should be shown when very high volume or pressure is used relative to compliance

### Recruitment with PEEP
- loop may shift in a way that suggests improved opening at modest PEEP
- if compliance improves, delivered volume per pressure may improve

### Leak
- inspiratory and expiratory limbs do not close properly
- loop may fail to return cleanly

The loop does not need to be mathematically perfect, but it should clearly teach these high-yield visual patterns.

# 5. How Setting Changes Should Affect the Simulation

## A. Tidal Volume Increase
If the student increases tidal volume:
- minute ventilation rises
- ETCO2 tends to fall over subsequent breaths
- peak pressure rises
- plateau pressure rises
- mean airway pressure may rise
- pressure-volume loop extends to higher volumes
- if compliance is poor, pressures rise disproportionately
- if overdone, overdistension alarm logic may be triggered

## B. Respiratory Rate Increase
If the student increases RR:
- minute ventilation rises if tidal volume is preserved
- ETCO2 tends to fall
- expiratory time shortens
- in obstructive physiology, air trapping may worsen
- flow may not return to zero
- auto-PEEP may increase
- severe over-rapid ventilation can reduce venous return and blood pressure modestly in susceptible states

## C. PEEP Increase
If the student increases PEEP:
- baseline airway pressure rises
- mean airway pressure rises
- oxygenation may improve if recruitable lung units are present
- blood pressure may fall if preload is limited
- SpO2 may improve slowly in recruitable states
- peak and plateau pressures may rise from baseline shift
- pressure-volume loop starts at a higher pressure environment

## D. FiO2 Increase
If FiO2 increases:
- oxygen reserve rises
- SpO2 may improve depending on pathology
- no immediate major change in pressure waveforms
- useful in hypoxemia, disconnect recovery, anaphylaxis, bronchospasm, and airway events

## E. Inspiratory Time Increase
If inspiratory time increases:
- mean airway pressure rises
- flow pattern changes
- pressure waveform may have a longer inspiratory plateau
- can help oxygenation modestly
- may worsen hemodynamics if excessive

## F. Inspiratory Pressure Increase in Pressure Control
If inspiratory pressure increases:
- delivered tidal volume rises if compliance/resistance allow
- minute ventilation rises
- ETCO2 tends to fall
- peak pressure rises to the new target
- if compliance is poor, volume gain may be limited

## G. Inspiratory Flow Increase in Volume Control
If inspiratory flow increases:
- inspiratory time shortens
- resistive component rises
- peak pressure increases more than plateau
- may be uncomfortable or mechanically harsh in some states

# 6. How Patient State Changes Should Affect the Simulation

## A. Low Compliance
Examples:
- pneumoperitoneum
- obesity
- pulmonary edema
- severe atelectasis

Effects:
- peak pressure increases
- plateau pressure increases
- pressure-volume loop shifts upward
- delivered tidal volume falls in pressure control
- oxygenation may worsen
- ETCO2 may rise if minute ventilation falls enough

## B. High Resistance
Examples:
- bronchospasm
- kinked tube
- secretions
- ETT obstruction

Effects:
- peak pressure increases
- plateau less elevated than peak
- peak-to-plateau gradient widens
- flow decay is prolonged
- auto-PEEP risk increases
- ETCO2 may rise
- capnogram may show obstructive upsloping / shark-fin pattern if bronchospasm is modeled

## C. Increased CO2 Production
Examples:
- fever
- shivering
- malignant hyperthermia-like state
- increased metabolism

Effects:
- ETCO2 rises over time
- minute ventilation requirement increases
- HR may increase
- temperature may rise in some scenarios

## D. Leak
Examples:
- cuff leak
- circuit leak
- partial disconnect

Effects:
- exhaled tidal volume falls
- minute ventilation may fall
- ETCO2 may decrease or become unreliable if severe
- alarm should trigger for low exhaled volume / disconnect
- SpO2 may eventually fall if severe

## E. Low Preload / Hypovolemia
Effects:
- arterial pressure falls
- ETCO2 may fall if cardiac output falls
- tachycardia may occur
- higher intrathoracic pressure from ventilation or PEEP may worsen hypotension

## F. Deep Anesthesia
Effects:
- BIS falls
- blood pressure may fall
- MAC / sevo may be elevated
- HR may be normal or slightly low depending on scenario

# 7. Cross-Talk Between Ventilation and Vital Signs

Students should explicitly learn that the ventilator and vital signs are not separate systems.

## A. Ventilation to ETCO2
Changes in minute ventilation should affect ETCO2 after a short delay of a few breaths.

## B. Ventilation to Blood Pressure
High intrathoracic pressure can reduce venous return.

Situations where BP may fall:
- excessive PEEP
- excessive mean airway pressure
- aggressive ventilation in hypovolemic patients

## C. Perfusion to ETCO2
If cardiac output abruptly falls:
- ETCO2 may fall even if ventilation is unchanged

This should be used in hypotension, embolic, or arrest-like scenario logic.

## D. Severe Airway Obstruction to SpO2 and ETCO2
With severe bronchospasm or obstruction:
- ETCO2 may rise initially
- capnogram becomes abnormal
- SpO2 falls later
- peak pressure rises

## E. Anaphylaxis to Multiple Systems
Anaphylaxis should influence:
- blood pressure
- heart rate
- bronchospasm / airway resistance
- ETCO2 waveform shape
- SpO2 over time
- ventilator pressures

---

# 8. Alarm Logic

## Required alarms and triggers

### High airway pressure
Trigger when peak inspiratory pressure exceeds a threshold.

### Low exhaled tidal volume / disconnect
Trigger when exhaled volume is significantly below inspired volume or below target.

### Low minute ventilation / apnea
Trigger when exhaled ventilation is inadequate.

### High ETCO2
Trigger when ETCO2 rises above threshold.

### Low ETCO2
Trigger when ETCO2 drops below threshold.
This may indicate hyperventilation, low perfusion, disconnect, or severe deterioration depending on context.

### Low FiO2
Trigger when delivered oxygen fraction falls below threshold.

### Optional high severity alarms
- severe desaturation
- severe hypotension
- possible anaphylaxis pattern
- circuit disconnect suspected
- bronchospasm suspected

Alarm wording should be educational and plausible.

---

# 9. BIS Logic

The BIS monitor should not just be decorative.

## BIS relationships
- deeper anesthetic state lowers BIS
- lighter state raises BIS
- BIS responds more slowly than instantaneous vital changes
- BIS should generally remain in a maintenance range under general anesthesia unless scenario logic changes it

### Approximate teaching ranges
- 80–100: awake / lightly sedated
- 60–80: moderate sedation to light anesthesia
- 40–60: common general anesthesia target
- below 40: deeper hypnotic state

The EEG trace and spectral heatmap should move continuously and reflect slower trends rather than random noise.

# 10. Common Anesthesia Scenarios Window

Create a **separate window / panel** containing **3 hard-coded common anesthesia scenarios**.

Each scenario should:
- initialize a starting patient state
- change vitals and mechanics over time
- present a multiple-choice next-step question
- update the patient state after each choice
- teach why the choice was correct or incorrect

This should function like a guided anesthesia simulation mini-case.

---

## Scenario Window Requirements

The scenario panel should include:

- scenario title
- short clinical context
- live monitor changes
- current question
- multiple choice answers
- immediate educational feedback
- next step progression
- scenario resolution state

Each scenario should have **several steps**, not just one question.

The vitals and ventilator waveforms should visibly change at every meaningful step.

---

# 11. Scenario 1: Intraoperative Hypotension After Induction

## Teaching goal
Teach students to recognize common causes of post-induction hypotension and take appropriate next steps.

## Starting state
- patient under general anesthesia after induction
- sevo / anesthetic depth present
- BP begins to fall
- HR may be normal or mildly elevated
- ventilator mechanics are mostly normal
- ETCO2 is normal or slightly reduced
- BIS may be in an acceptable anesthetic range

## Initial monitor pattern
- arterial pressure trends downward
- NIBP confirms hypotension
- SpO2 remains normal
- ETCO2 may fall slightly if cardiac output drops
- ventilator waveforms remain mostly normal

## Example first question
**The patient becomes hypotensive shortly after induction. What is the best next step?**

Possible answers:
- A. Increase tidal volume
- B. Give a vasopressor and assess likely cause
- C. Decrease FiO2
- D. Ignore because SpO2 is normal

Correct answer:
- **B**

## If correct
- blood pressure improves
- ETCO2 may recover slightly
- scenario explains vasodilation / anesthetic depth / preload effects

## If incorrect
Examples:
- increasing tidal volume may not fix the cause and may slightly worsen venous return
- decreasing FiO2 is clearly wrong
- ignoring is unsafe

## Additional steps
Possible follow-up questions:
- patient remains hypotensive; what do you assess next?
- should you decrease volatile, give fluid, or give additional pressor?
- what if ETCO2 is also falling?

This scenario should teach:
- induction-related vasodilation
- preload sensitivity
- role of volatile anesthetics
- relationship between BP and ETCO2

---

# 12. Scenario 2: Anaphylaxis to Rocuronium

## Teaching goal
Teach recognition of peri-induction anaphylaxis and the correct immediate response.

## Starting state
Shortly after rocuronium:
- blood pressure falls sharply
- heart rate rises
- airway resistance rises
- peak pressures rise
- capnogram may show bronchospastic pattern
- SpO2 begins to decline over time
- ETCO2 may vary depending on bronchospasm and perfusion
- skin findings can be described in text even if not visualized

## Monitor pattern
- severe hypotension
- tachycardia
- high peak pressure
- possible prolonged expiratory flow
- possible obstructive ETCO2 morphology
- eventual oxygenation decline

## Example first question
**The patient becomes hypotensive with rising airway pressures shortly after rocuronium. What is the best immediate next step?**

Possible answers:
- A. Give epinephrine and 100% oxygen
- B. Increase sevoflurane
- C. Lower PEEP only
- D. Wait for blood pressure cuff recycle

Correct answer:
- **A**

## If correct
- FiO2 goes to 100%
- epinephrine improves BP over the next moments
- bronchospasm gradually improves
- peak pressures fall
- SpO2 stabilizes or improves
- scenario can prompt for fluids and calling for help next

## If incorrect
- patient worsens
- hypotension persists
- SpO2 falls further
- alarms intensify

## Additional steps
Follow-up questions may include:
- what else should be done after epinephrine?
  - fluids
  - stop suspected trigger
  - call for help
  - consider adjuncts
- what monitor changes suggest improving bronchospasm?

This scenario should teach:
- anaphylaxis pattern recognition
- epinephrine as first-line
- oxygen and support
- bronchospasm + hypotension combination

---

# 13. Scenario 3: Bronchospasm After Airway Instrumentation

## Teaching goal
Teach obstructive mechanics and the relationship between resistance, capnography, and ventilator pressures.

## Starting state
After airway stimulation:
- peak pressure rises
- flow does not return to zero fully
- ETCO2 rises
- capnogram develops obstructive upsloping
- SpO2 may still be normal initially
- BP may be stable or mildly elevated
- HR may increase

## Example first question
**Peak pressures are rising and the capnogram shows an obstructive pattern. What is the best next step?**

Possible answers:
- A. Give bronchodilator and deepen anesthesia
- B. Increase respiratory rate significantly
- C. Ignore because oxygen saturation is normal
- D. Decrease FiO2

Correct answer:
- **A**

## If correct
- resistance gradually falls
- peak pressure decreases
- expiratory flow returns closer to zero
- ETCO2 improves over time
- capnogram normalizes

## If incorrect
- excessive RR worsens air trapping
- ignoring allows progression
- lower FiO2 is clearly harmful

## Additional steps
Possible next questions:
- what ventilator change helps obstructive physiology?
  - longer expiratory time
  - avoid stacking breaths
- what waveform indicates improvement?
  - expiratory flow returning to zero

This scenario should teach:
- high resistance mechanics
- capnogram recognition
- avoiding breath stacking
- bronchodilator / deeper anesthesia approach

# 14. Multiple Choice Interaction Rules

Each scenario question should have:

- 1 best answer
- 3 distractors that are plausible enough to teach with
- immediate feedback
- explanation of why the choice helped or harmed
- monitor/vitals change after answer selection

## Good answer behavior
If a correct answer is selected:
- patient state improves in the proper direction
- waveforms change over several seconds / breaths
- not everything instantly normalizes unless the intervention truly should

## Incorrect answer behavior
If an incorrect answer is selected:
- state either worsens or fails to improve
- explanation is given
- learner can either retry or continue with consequences

## Educational feedback should explain
- what clue on the monitors mattered most
- why the chosen action was or was not appropriate
- which waveform or vital sign should improve if the right therapy is given

# 15. Scenario State Engine

The scenario system should be hard-coded but modular.

Each scenario should include:

- `id`
- `title`
- `summary`
- `initialPatientState`
- `timelineChanges`
- `questionSteps`
- `correctEffects`
- `incorrectEffects`
- `resolutionState`

Each step can modify:
- HR
- BP
- SpO2
- ETCO2
- RR
- compliance
- resistance
- leak
- ventilator pressures
- BIS
- alarm state

This lets monitors visibly respond to choices.

# 16. Example of Educational Directionality Students Should Learn

## Hypotension
Students should learn:
- volatile anesthetics can reduce BP
- vasodilation and low preload are common causes
- ETCO2 may fall when cardiac output falls
- aggressive positive pressure can worsen venous return

## Bronchospasm
Students should learn:
- peak pressure rises
- expiratory flow is prolonged
- ETCO2 waveform shape changes
- increasing RR can worsen air trapping

## Low compliance
Students should learn:
- both peak and plateau rise
- pressure control may deliver lower tidal volumes
- PEEP and recruitment may help some states
- oxygenation may worsen

## Leak / disconnect
Students should learn:
- exhaled tidal volume falls
- ETCO2 may fall
- alarms matter
- waveforms and volume return become inconsistent

## Anaphylaxis
Students should learn:
- hypotension + bronchospasm is a dangerous combination
- epinephrine and oxygen are immediate priorities
- fluids and help are also important
- monitors improve in the right sequence, not all at once
